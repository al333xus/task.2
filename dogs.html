Overview

Why activity and routine care matter

The domestic dog is an extremely social animal and offers a wide range of breed options. Many studies cite social benefits of dog companionship, reduced loneliness, and improved health indicators. This page reorganizes the original long text into clearer sections for exercise, grooming, and preventive care.

Diet & Exercise

Throughout a dog’s lifespan, dogs should generally be fed fewer meals as they mature. Puppies should be nursed for the first two months, then introduced to three meals per day from about three to six months old. Food intake should be reduced to two meals per day from six months to one year old and finally to one meal after one year old.

A dog’s typical body mass is composed of about 60 to 70 percent water. The ASPCA says a 10 percent decrease in body water can cause illness, and a 15 percent loss can cause death, so hydration is essential.

Dogs also need healthy activity levels, sunlight, and fresh air. The minimum recommended exercise time varies by breed and size, but a common range is 30 to 120 minutes per day. Brisk walks count, and toys that encourage physical activity can help when they are age-appropriate and safe.

Grooming & Handling

Brush your dog frequently to reduce shedding and prevent matted fur. Check for insects such as ticks and fleas, especially after visiting heavily wooded areas. When bathing, rinse all soap out completely because residue can cause a rash. Use soap formulated for dogs because harsh soaps can cause allergic reactions.

Be careful when carrying your dog. For a small dog, cradle the pup with one hand under the chest and the forearm supporting the back half. For a large dog, reach under the belly with both arms perpendicular to the dog and support the chest and rear.

Vaccinations & Medications

You should always consult with your veterinarian, but there is a core set of vaccines that are typically recommended. These reduce exposure risk to conditions such as hepatitis, rabies, and parvovirus. Your veterinarian may also recommend additional vaccinations depending on the environment in which your dog lives, including Bordetella bronchiseptica, Borrelia burgdorferi, and Leptospira bacteria.
