Overview

Understanding cat nutrition and early growth

Cats were first domesticated around 7500 BCE in western Asia and are currently the second most popular domestic pet in the United States. While there are over 60 documented cat breeds, diet and care needs can vary by breed and life stage. The guidance below keeps the content easier to scan while preserving the required information.

Cat owners are looking to understand feeding needs based on age, type of cat, diet, and other preferences. This page uses shorter sections and direct calls to action to support that persona.

Zero to Four Weeks

It is important that the queen directly nurse her young if possible. Monitor kitten growth closely to make sure the growth rate is progressing steadily. If a kitten is not growing at a sufficient rate, a caretaker might need to feed the kitten directly with a bottle or a feeding tube.

  • Too many siblings are competing for mom’s milk.
  • Gastrointestinal disease can affect growth.
  • Environmental conditions such as extreme heat, cold, or unsanitary conditions can interfere with development.

Four Weeks to One Year

Kittens can typically start being introduced to soft wet kitten food around three to four weeks after birth. According to the ASPCA, kittens at this age should eat half to one cup of dry kitten food or six to nine ounces of wet kitten food per day. If hard food is difficult to eat, a small amount of water can soften it.

Food optimized for kittens provides the additional nutrients needed for growth, energy, and wellness. Breed-specific needs can vary, so veterinary guidance is still important.

One Year to Seven Years

At this stage, kittens have reached the adult cat stage and do not need as many nutrients. Their activity level and metabolism decrease. It is not recommended to leave food out all day. Instead, provide meals a couple of times per day rather than allowing continual snacking.

Seven Years and More

As cats age, their bodies experience more changes. Older cats should generally eat fewer fats and calories but more quality proteins. When purchasing packaged foods, look for products that name a specific protein, such as salmon, rather than a broad category such as fish.
